In Sūrah al-Zumar, Allah
Most High says:

<div dir="rtl">
أَمَّنْ هُوَ قَـٰنِتٌ ءَانَآءَ ٱلَّيْلِ
سَاجِدًۭا وَقَآئِمًۭا يَحْذَرُ ٱلْـَٔاخِرَةَ وَيَرْجُوا۟ رَحْمَةَ رَبِّهِۦ ۗ
قُلْ هَلْ يَسْتَوِى ٱلَّذِينَ يَعْلَمُونَ وَٱلَّذِينَ لَا يَعْلَمُونَ ۗ
إِنَّمَا يَتَذَكَّرُ أُو۟لُوا۟ ٱلْأَلْبَـٰبِ
</div>

{Or \[is it] the one who worships
devoutly during the night, prostrating and standing, while being wary of the life
to come and hoping for the mercy of his Lord? Say, ‘Are those who know equal to
those who do not know?’ Only those of pure intellect take heed}  (Qurʾān 39:9).

ʿAllāmah Abū al-Barakāt al-Nasafī (may Allah have mercy on him) says in Madārik al-Tanzīl wa Ḥaqāʾiq al-Taʾwīl:

![Tafsir al-Nasafi - Surah al-Zumar (a)](/images/al-nasafi-zumar-a.jpeg "Tafsir al-Nasafi - Surah al-Zumar (a)")

{Or \[is it] the one} Makiyy, Nāfiʿ, and Hamzah read it with takhfīf as the interrogative particle أ preceding من (who), and the others \[recite it] with tashdīd as the conjunctive particle أم (or) preceding it. من (who) is the subject, and its predicate is omitted. Its implicit \[meaning] is:

“Is the one {who worships devoutly}
like the other people?” I.e., is the who is obedient like the one who is rebellious?
The word al-qāniṭ means ‘the one who is obedient to God.’ The predicate is omitted
because its meaning is implied by the discourse, which is in the context of
discussing unbelievers in the previous āyah and then His Word {Say, ‘Are those
who know equal to those who do not know?’}. 

![Tafsir al-Nasafi - Surah al-Zumar (b)](/images/al-nasafi-zumar-b.jpeg "Tafsir al-Nasafi - Surah al-Zumar (b)")



{During the night} signifies
its hours. The words {prostrating and standing} are in the grammatical position
of حال (accusative of circumstance), which describes
the state of the pronoun embedded within the word al-qāniṭ (the devout). 

 

{While being wary of the
life to come} refers to the punishment of the afterlife. {And hoping for the
for the mercy of his Lord} refers to Paradise.

 

This āyah shows that it is
necessary for the believer to be between the states of khawf (fear) and rajāʾ
(hope). He has hope in the mercy of his Lord rather than relying on his own
actions. He is wary of its torment due to deficiencies in his deeds. Consequently,
when rajāʾ goes beyond its limit, there is an exaggerated sense of safety, and
when khawf goes beyond its limit, it leads to excessive feelings of despair.
Allah Most High says, {None feels safe from Allah’s devising except for the people
who lose} (Qurʾān 7:99), and, {None despairs from Allah’s relief except for the
unbelieving people} (Qurʾān 12:87). Therefore, it is necessary that each state does
not exceed its limit.

 

{Say, ‘Are those who know
equal to those who do not know?’} It refers to those who have knowledge and to
live according to it since because who do not put knowledge into practice would
be considered something other than an ʿālim (scholar). That contains an immense
warning for those who attain (يقتنون - form VIII: qāf nūn waaw) knowledge of the Islamic
sciences and do not become devout (يقنُتون – form I: qāf nūn tāʾ) and for those who become a specialist
in the field (يفتنون –
Form VIII from fāʾ nūn nūn) and remain enchanted (يفتنون – passive of Form I from fāʾ tāʾ nūn) by the Dunya. Such
people are ignorant about Allah in that only the virtuous can be considered ʿulamāʾ
(scholars).

 

Alternatively, the āyah can
be interpreted as tashbīh (a simile) to express how a scholar and someone who
is ignorant are not equal just as a virtuous person and someone who is disobedient
are not equal.

 

{Only those of pure intellect
take heed.} The word albāb (intellects) is the plural of lubb (intellect). It expresses
the idea that the only people who truly heed Allah’s admonition are those who
possess pure intellects.
